The [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirkpatrick%E2%80%93Seidel_algorithm '''Kirkpatrick–Seidel algorithm'''] is a computational geometry algorithm that efficiently computes the convex hull of a set of points in the plane. Sometimes referred to as the "ultimate convex hull algorithm," it achieves an optimal time complexity of <math>O(n \log h)</math>, where <math>n</math> is the number of input points and <math>h</math> is the number of points on the convex hull. This algorithm is notable for its "divide-and-conquer" approach, which differs significantly from other convex hull algorithms like Graham's scan or Jarvis march.

;Overview
The Kirkpatrick–Seidel algorithm divides the input points into subsets recursively in a way that simplifies the computation of the convex hull. It uses a vertical line to split the points into left and right subsets, and then processes these subsets independently to compute the upper and lower convex hulls. The results are merged to form the complete convex hull of the input points.

;Steps of the Algorithm
# '''Divide the points''': A vertical line is chosen to divide the set of points into two subsets—those to the left and those to the right of the line.
# '''Find the median x-coordinate''': The median <math>x</math>-coordinate is used as the dividing line, ensuring that the subsets are roughly equal in size.
# '''Compute bridges''': A "bridge" is a line segment connecting two points—one from each subset—that lies above all other points. Finding the bridge is a critical step in constructing the convex hull.
# '''Recurse''': The algorithm continues by recursively finding the bridges for the left and right subsets.
# '''Merge''': The upper and lower hulls are computed separately and merged to form the final convex hull.

;Key Properties
The Kirkpatrick–Seidel algorithm is efficient because it focuses on the points that are part of the convex hull and disregards the rest. By leveraging the divide-and-conquer approach, it processes only a small subset of the points in each recursive step. The complexity of <math>O(n \log h)</math> makes it particularly well-suited for cases where <math>h</math>, the number of hull points, is much smaller than <math>n</math>.

;Applications
The Kirkpatrick–Seidel algorithm is used in various applications of computational geometry, including:
* Geographic Information Systems (GIS)
* Computer graphics
* Pattern recognition
* Robotics and motion planning
