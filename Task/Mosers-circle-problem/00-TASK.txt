Moser's circle problem investigates the maximum number of distinct regions obtained by picking <math>n</math> random points on a circle and drawing chords between them. By necessity, to produce the maximum number, the points must be chosen so no three chords intersect at the same point.

A circle with one point, and chords drawn to every other point (none), has one region.

With two points, and chords connecting them, it has two regions.

With three points, four.

With four points, eight.

With five points, sixteen.

It seems to be following a steady geometric progression, <math>f(n)=2^n</math>, but the sequence breaks at six. Six points, with chords drawn between them yields thirty-'''one''', not thirty-'''two''' regions.


A direct equation for solving Moser's circle problem for any <math>n</math>:

<math>f(n) = \frac{1}{24}(n^4 - 6n^3 + 23n^2 - 18n + 24)</math>

But there are many other ways to solve for <math>n</math>,

using binomials, (<math> f(n)={n \choose 4}+{n \choose 2}+1</math>),

binomial transform (binomial transform of 5 1s followed by infinite 0s)

or Pascals triangle (sum the first five values of row <math>n</math>)

among others.
