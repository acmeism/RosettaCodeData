The '''nimbers''', also known as '''Grundy''' numbers, are the values of the heaps in the game of [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nim Nim]. They have '''addition''' and '''multiplication''' operations, unrelated to the addition and multiplication of the integers. Both operations are defined recursively:

The '''nim-sum''' of two integers {{math|<var>m</var>}} and {{math|<var>n</var>}}, denoted {{math|<var>m&oplus;n</var>}} is given by

{{math|<var>m&oplus;n</var>&#61;mex(<var>m'&oplus;n, m&oplus; n' : m'<m, n'<n</var>)}},

where the mex function returns the smallest integer ''not'' in the set. More simply: collect all the nim-sums of ''m'' and numbers smaller than ''n'', and all nim-sums of ''n'' with all numbers less than ''m'' and find the smallest number not in that set. Fortunately, this also turns out to be equal to the bitwise xor of the two.

The '''nim-product''' is also defined recursively:

{{math|<var>m&otimes;n</var>&#61;mex([<var>m'&otimes;n</var>]&oplus;[<var>m&otimes;n'</var>]&oplus;[<var>m'&otimes;n'</var>] : <var>m'<m, n'<n</var>)}}

The product is more complicated and time-consuming to evaluate, but there are a few facts which may help:
*The operators {{math|&oplus;}} and {{math|&otimes;}} are commutative and distributive
*the nim-product of a Fermat power ({{math|2<sup>2<sup><var>k</var></sup></sup>}}) and a smaller number is their ordinary product
*the nim-square of a Fermat power {{math|<var>x</var>}} is the ordinary product {{math|3<var>x</var>/2}}
