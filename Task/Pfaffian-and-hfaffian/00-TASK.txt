[[Category:Matrices]]
For a given matrix, return the [[wp:Pfaffian|Pfaffian]] and the [[wp:Hafnian|hfaffian]] of the matrix.

The Pfaffian is given by
:: <big><math>\operatorname{Pf}(A) = \frac{1}{2^n n!} \sum_{\sigma \in S_{2n}} \sgn(\sigma) \prod_{i=1}^n A_{\sigma(2i-1), \sigma(2i)}</math></big>
while the hfaffian is given by
:: <big><math>\operatorname{hf}(A) = \frac{1}{2^n n!} \sum_{\sigma \in S_{2n}} \prod_{i=1}^n A_{\sigma(2i-1), \sigma(2i)}</math></big>
In both cases the sum is over the permutations <math>\sigma</math> of the symmetric group on <math>2n</math> elements. (A permutation's sign is 1 if there are an even number of inversions and -1 otherwise; see [[wp:Parity of a permutation|parity of a permutation]].)

Efficient methods for calculating the Pfaffian are known: see for example [[wp:Pfaffian#Efficient computation]]. However, efficient methods for calculating the hfaffian are not known.

;FYI<nowiki>:</nowiki> How Many Definitions Does the Pfaffian Have?

The '''Pfaffian''' has multiple definitions depending on the context and application. Below are the most common definitions of the Pfaffian:

;1. Definition via Skew-Symmetric Matrices
The Pfaffian is often defined as an algebraic object associated with a <math>2n \times 2n</math> '''skew-symmetric matrix'''. Let <math>A</math> be a <math>2n \times 2n</math> skew-symmetric matrix (i.e., <math>A^T = -A</math>). The Pfaffian is defined such that:

<math>\text{det}(A) = \text{Pf}(A)^2</math>

This means that the determinant of <math>A</math> is the square of its Pfaffian. This is one of the most fundamental definitions, as it highlights the relationship between the Pfaffian and the determinant.

;2. Definition via Summation
The Pfaffian can also be defined explicitly as a summation over permutations. Let <math>A = (a_{ij})</math> be a <math>2n \times 2n</math> skew-symmetric matrix. The Pfaffian is then given by:

<math>\text{Pf}(A) = \frac{1}{n! 2^n} \sum_{\sigma \in S_{2n}} \text{sgn}(\sigma) \prod_{i=1}^n a_{\sigma(2i-1), \sigma(2i)}</math>

Where:
* <math>S_{2n}</math> is the symmetric group (the set of all permutations of <math>2n</math> elements),
* <math>\text{sgn}(\sigma)</math> is the sign of the permutation <math>\sigma</math>,
* The product <math>\prod_{i=1}^n a_{\sigma(2i-1), \sigma(2i)}</math> ensures pairwise contributions.

In practice, the summation is computed over specific subsets of pairings to reduce complexity.

;3. Recursive Definition
The Pfaffian can also be defined recursively. For a <math>2n \times 2n</math> skew-symmetric matrix <math>A</math>, the recursion is as follows:

* For <math>n = 1</math> (<math>A</math> is a <math>2 \times 2</math> matrix):
  <math>\text{Pf}(A) = a_{12}</math>
* For <math>n > 1</math>:
  <math>\text{Pf}(A) = \sum_{j=2}^{2n} (-1)^j a_{1j} \cdot \text{Pf}(A_{1j}),</math>

where <math>A_{1j}</math> is the submatrix obtained by removing the 1st row and <math>j</math>-th column of <math>A</math>.

This recursive definition is theoretically useful but computationally less efficient.

;4. Definition in Graph Theory
In graph theory, the Pfaffian is often used to count ''perfect matchings'' in a graph. For a graph <math>G</math> with <math>2n</math> vertices, one can define a skew-symmetric adjacency matrix <math>A</math>, where the entries of <math>A</math> encode the graph's edges and weights. The Pfaffian of <math>A</math> is closely related to the number of ''perfect matchings'' in <math>G</math>.

If <math>G</math> is a planar graph, and the weights satisfy specific conditions, then:

<math>\text{Pf}(A) = \pm \text{Number of Perfect Matchings in } G</math>

This definition is widely used in combinatorics, statistical mechanics, and the dimer model.

;5. Definition via Exterior Algebra
In the language of exterior algebra, the Pfaffian can be interpreted geometrically. Let <math>A</math> be a <math>2n \times 2n</math> skew-symmetric matrix, and define a 2-form <math>\omega</math> as:

<math>\omega = \sum_{i<j} a_{ij} dx_i \wedge dx_j</math>

Then the <math>n</math>-th wedge power of <math>\omega</math> satisfies:

<math>\omega^n = \text{Pf}(A) \cdot (dx_1 \wedge dx_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx_{2n})</math>

This interpretation highlights the Pfaffian as a volume form in exterior algebra and differential geometry.

;6. Definition via Block Matrices
If <math>A</math> is a <math>2n \times 2n</math> skew-symmetric matrix, it can sometimes be expressed in terms of block matrices:

<math>A = \begin{bmatrix}
0 & B \\
-B^T & 0
\end{bmatrix}</math>

In this case, the Pfaffian can sometimes be defined or computed using properties of the block <math>B</math>. This approach is particularly useful in physics and statistical mechanics.

;Summary
The Pfaffian has several equivalent definitions, which can be categorized as follows:
# '''Algebraic Definition''': Defined via the determinant as <math>\text{det}(A) = \text{Pf}(A)^2</math>.
# '''Combinatorial Definition''': Defined as a summation over permutations.
# '''Recursive Definition''': Defined using submatrices and recursion.
# '''Graph Theory Definition''': Related to ''perfect matchings'' in graphs.
# '''Geometric Definition''': Defined via exterior algebra and differential forms.
# '''Block Matrix Definition''': Using the structure of <math>2n \times 2n</math> skew-symmetric matrices.

Each definition arises naturally in different mathematical and physical contexts, and they are all fundamentally equivalent.
