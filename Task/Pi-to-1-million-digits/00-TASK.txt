Even before Sir Isaac Newton calculated π to 16 decimal places by hand in 1666 while at home during the bubonic plague epidemic, there has been an ongoing quest to compute the mathematical constant to ever more digits.

It was the development of computers in the mid-20th century which revolutionized the hunt for digits of π:
<br><br>• Mathematicians John Wrench and Levi Smith reached 1,120 digits in 1949 using a desk calculator.
<br><br>• Using an inverse tangent (arctan) infinite series, a team led by George Reitwiesner and John von Neumann that same year achieved 2,037 digits with a calculation that took 70 hours of computer time on the ENIAC computer.
<br><br>• The record, always relying on an arctan series, was broken repeatedly: 3089 digits in 1955, 7,480 digits in 1957; 10,000 digits in 1958; 100,000 digits in 1961; and 500,000 digits in 1967.
<br><br>A landmark in the calculation of π came in 1973 when Jean Guilloud and Martine Bouyer, using a CDC 7600 supercomputer, computed π to one million digits (1,001,250 to be exact) in 23 hours.

However, those achievements pale in significance compared with an effort that took 226 days resulting in the calculation of π to 300,000,000,000,000 (300 trillion) digits. That feat was completed on April 2, 2025 by the Linus Media Group Inc. (LMG), a privately held Canadian digital media entertainment company. It set a Guinness World Record for its achievement. A YouTube video commemorating the feat can be viewed at: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BD-AJwqzWsU Calculating Pi to 300 trillion digits]

While it's probably beyond the ability of most Rosetta Code contributors to calculate π to 300 trillion places, with today's computers π can be calculated to a million digits often in milliseconds rather than the 23 hours it took in 1973.
