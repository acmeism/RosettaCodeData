Shuffle groups are integers that, when the digits are rearranged, form an exact even multiple of the original number. 

For instance, the integer '''11883''' can be rearranged to form '''83181''' which is exactly '''7''' times '''11883'''. The multiple '''7''' is known as a "witness" to the '''11883''' shuffle group.

Some integers can have multiple witnesses. '''123876''' has two: '''3''' ('''371628''') and '''7''' ('''867132''').

Integers that end in zero are not considered to be the base of a shuffle group, since they can be generated trivially by adding zeros to the end of any other shuffle group.

Shuffle groups are typically displayed by showing the base integer and the witnesses (multiples) that form that group.
